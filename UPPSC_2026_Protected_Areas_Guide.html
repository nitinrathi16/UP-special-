Categories of Protected Areas — legal status & human activity

🛡️

Four overlapping designations form India's conservation backbone. The first two are legal categories under the Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972; the latter two are conservation frameworks that overlay existing protected areas.

🌲
Legal · WLPA 1972

National Park

Declared byState Govt (notification)
ProtectionHighest / strictest
Human activityNo grazing, no rights; tightly regulated
BoundariesFixed & legally defined
FirstHailey (now Jim Corbett), 1936

Declared byState Govt
ProtectionHigh, but more flexible than NP

BoundariesNot as strictly fixed
NoteA sanctuary can be upgraded to an NP
🌍
Framework · UNESCO MAB

Biosphere Reserve

Recognised byGovt of India; UNESCO (MAB)
ProtectionZoned: core / buffer / transition
Human activityNone in core; allowed in buffer & transition
AimConservation + sustainable development + research
Largest unitEncloses NPs & sanctuaries
🐅
Framework · Project Tiger

Tiger Reserve

Notified byState, on NTCA recommendation
ProtectionCore (inviolate) + buffer (multi-use)
Human activityNone in core/CTH; regulated in buffer
Legal hookWLPA 1972 (2006 amendment) + NTCA
StartedProject Tiger, 1973
One-line memory hook: Sanctuary → species-focused & flexible · National Park → ecosystem-focused & strict · Biosphere Reserve → people + nature, zoned · Tiger Reserve → a management overlay with an inviolate core.
Exam trap: A Biosphere Reserve has no independent legal status of its own — the legal protection comes from the National Parks and Sanctuaries that sit inside it. Similarly, a Ramsar tag is an international recognition, not domestic law.

Tiger Reserves & Project Tiger

🐅

Project Tiger launched in 1973 with 9 reserves. It is administered by the National Tiger Conservation Authority (NTCA), a statutory body under the WLPA (2006 amendment), within the Ministry of Environment, Forest & Climate Change.

Headline facts (verify-fresh, June 2026):
• Total: 58 tiger reserves across ~18 states.
• First: Palamau (Jharkhand) era of 1973; Latest: Madhav (MP), notified March 2025 as the 58th.
• Largest: Nagarjunsagar-Srisailam (Andhra Pradesh).
• Most reserves in a state: Madhya Pradesh (9) — the "Tiger State".
• Highest tiger population (2022): Madhya Pradesh (785).
Structure of a tiger reserve: a core / Critical Tiger Habitat (CTH) kept inviolate, surrounded by a buffer permitting regulated human use. Census uses the "double sampling" method — ground signs (pugmarks, scat) plus camera-trap stripe-pattern ID, since every tiger's stripes are unique. The 6th cycle of the All-India Tiger Estimation is underway in 2026.

🟧 Uttar Pradesh's Tiger Reserves

UP has three tiger reserves: Dudhwa, Pilibhit, and the newer Ranipur (Chitrakoot). Dudhwa and Pilibhit are the exam staples.

🐅
Terai · Lakhimpur Kheri

Dudhwa Tiger Reserve

Created by clubbing Dudhwa NP + Kishanpur & Katarniaghat sanctuaries. Lies in the Terai grasslands along the Indo-Nepal border, watered by the Sharda, Suheli & Mohana rivers.

Flagship speciesTiger, swamp deer (barasingha), one-horned rhino (reintroduced)
EcosystemTerai grassland + sal forest + wetlands
SpecialSuccessful rhino reintroduction programme
🐅
Terai · Pilibhit district

Pilibhit Tiger Reserve

Notified in 2014 on the Indo-Nepal border, part of the Terai Arc Landscape. Drained by the Sharda & Gomti rivers and the Sharda Sagar reservoir.

Flagship speciesTiger, swamp deer, leopard, hispid hare
AwardFirst-ever TX2 Award (2020) for doubling tigers
EcosystemTerai grassland + plantation sal forest
UP quick-fire: Dudhwa = UP's only National Park & oldest tiger reserve · Pilibhit = TX2 award winner · Ranipur (Chitrakoot) = UP's newest (notified 2022) · All three sit in or near the Terai Arc Landscape, a tiger-corridor shared with Nepal.

Biosphere Reserves & UNESCO recognition

🌍

India has 18 notified Biosphere Reserves. 12 are internationally recognised under UNESCO's Man and the Biosphere (MAB) World Network — with Cold Desert (HP) the most recent addition, which some 2026 sources count to take the UNESCO tally toward 13. Each reserve is zoned into core, buffer & transition.

UNESCO · 2000 (1st)

🏔️ Nilgiri

TN–Kerala–Karnataka. India's first Biosphere Reserve (1986) & first UNESCO-listed. Nilgiri tahr, lion-tailed macaque.

UNESCO · 2001

🐠 Gulf of Mannar

Tamil Nadu. India's first marine Biosphere Reserve — coral reefs, seagrass, dugong.

UNESCO · 2001

🌊 Sundarbans

West Bengal. World's largest mangrove; Royal Bengal tiger, also a Ramsar site & WHS.

UNESCO · 2001

🦏 Manas

Assam. Tiger reserve + WHS + Biosphere; golden langur, pygmy hog.

UNESCO · 2004

⛰️ Nanda Devi

Uttarakhand. High-Himalayan; snow leopard, Himalayan musk deer.

UNESCO · 2009

🌳 Pachmarhi

Madhya Pradesh (Satpura). Sal & teak; tiger, Indian giant squirrel.

UNESCO · 2009

🌿 Nokrek

Meghalaya. Red panda, citrus-indica gene pool.

UNESCO · 2009

🐅 Simlipal

Odisha. Tiger reserve; melanistic (black) tigers.

UNESCO · 2012

🌺 Agasthyamalai

Kerala–Tamil Nadu (W. Ghats). Endemic flora, tiger.

UNESCO · 2013

🏞️ Great Nicobar

A&N Islands. Leatherback turtle, Nicobar megapode.

UNESCO · 2016

🌲 Amarkantak

Achanakmar–Amarkantak (MP–Chhattisgarh). Source of the Narmada.

UNESCO · 2018

🏔️ Khangchendzonga

Sikkim. Snow leopard, red panda; also a mixed WHS.

First four UNESCO BRs (all 2000–01): Nilgiri · Gulf of Mannar · Sundarbans · Manas → mnemonic "Nilgiri Gobbles Sunlit Mangoes."
Not yet UNESCO-listed (notified only): Dibru-Saikhowa (Assam), Dehang-Debang (Arunachal), Great Rann of Kutch (Gujarat — largest by area), Seshachalam (AP), Panna (MP) & Cold Desert (HP). Watch list: dates and UNESCO-status pairings are a favourite UPPSC trap — Cold Desert's 2025 recognition is the newest update.

Ramsar Wetlands of International Importance

🦩

The Ramsar Convention (signed 1971, Ramsar, Iran; in force 1975) protects wetlands. India acceded on 1 Feb 1982. As of June 2026 India has 100 Ramsar sites — the 100th being Surha Tal / Jai Prakash Narayan Bird Sanctuary, Ballia (UP). India ranks 3rd in the world (after UK & Mexico) and 1st in Asia.

Concept trap: A Ramsar designation is an international label, not a domestic legal protection. The site is protected only if it is also notified under Indian law (e.g., as a sanctuary). Keoladeo, for instance, is a National Park and a Ramsar site and a World Heritage Site.

Biodiversity Hotspots in India

🌺

A "biodiversity hotspot" (Norman Myers' criteria) needs ≥1,500 endemic plant species and must have lost ≥70% of its original habitat. India falls within four of the world's 36 hotspots.

⛰️
Hotspot 1

The Himalayas

Eastern + Western Himalaya. Snow leopard, red panda, Himalayan musk deer, blue poppy. Spans Uttarakhand, Sikkim, Arunachal.

🌴
Hotspot 2

Western Ghats & Sri Lanka

Highest endemism in India — lion-tailed macaque, Nilgiri tahr, Malabar civet. A UNESCO World Heritage region.

🌿
Hotspot 3

Indo-Burma

India's Northeast (beyond the Himalaya) into Myanmar. Hoolock gibbon, pygmy hog, many freshwater turtles. Largest of the four.

🏝️
Hotspot 4

Sundaland (Nicobar Islands)

The Nicobar Islands fall in Sundaland. Nicobar megapode, leatherback turtle, saltwater crocodile.

Mnemonic — "HI-WIN": Himalaya · Indo-Burma · Western Ghats · sundaland (Nicobar). Remember: Andaman Islands are NOT a separate hotspot — only the Nicobar Islands (Sundaland) qualify.
